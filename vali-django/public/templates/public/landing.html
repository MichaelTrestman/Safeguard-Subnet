The SafeGuard Way

SafeGuard is a decentralized safety research engine for artificial intelligence services. It continuously probes live AI — chatbots, coding agents, enterprise assistants, customer-service models — for the ways they can harm or fail their users.

Distributed red-teamers compete to find new ways AIs fail. Network operators curate the threat models. Humans adjudicate the edge cases where automated judges disagree. AI vendors or researchers register targets for rolling testing or orchestrated experiments.

The threat models are public. Browse them here → They grow every cycle. The longer SafeGuard runs, the more ways-AIs-fail it has seen and turned into reusable, classifier-backed tests.

Built on Bittensor

SafeGuard runs as a subnet on Bittensor, a decentralized network where open economic roles replace the single-vendor model. No one company owns the concern catalog, the target list, or the scoring rubric. Three open roles keep the network running, and every contribution is paid on chain in TAO.

Miners

The Red-Teamers

Probe miners fetch the active concern catalog, pick an angle of attack on a registered target, and conduct multi-turn adversarial conversations through the relay. Baseline pay for findings the audit agrees with; a discovery bonus for novel cases a human later confirms. Anyone can run one.

Subject Matter Experts (humans in the loop)

Human input is sought to resolve judgment divergences in AI models, and to provide new input to grow, strengthen, and refine to threat model.

Validators

The Curators

Validators author and version concerns, dispatch probes to miners, run the audit pipeline, route edge cases to human adjudicators, and submit chain weights. Concern curation is safety research — validator operators decide what the whole network is looking for.

Delegators

The Underwriters

Anyone holding TAO can stake on a SafeGuard validator and earn a share of network emissions. Delegation is the capital layer of the network — it's how the infrastructure pays for itself without a sales team, a single paying customer, or permission from a gatekeeper. No KYC, no custody, no whitelist.

Why Bittensor as the substrate? A single company running the world's shared AI failure catalog has the same problem as a single company running the DNS root: the architecture has to be open for the catalog to be trusted, and the incentives have to pay in something other than one company's payroll budget.

How It Works

Step 01

Threat Models

Each threat model starts with a concern — a versioned natural language hypothesis about how an AI can fail — and a set of behaviors: specific, falsifiable descriptions of what that failure looks like in a transcript. Behaviors are scored by the HarmBench classifier classifier; concerns are audited by diverse AI judges. Both layers grow richer as probe data accumulates.

Step 02

Miners Probe Through The Relay

Probe miners fetch the active concern catalog, pick an angle of attack, and conduct a multi-turn adversarial conversation through SafeGuard's relay. Every target response is cryptographically committed before the miner sees it to prevent fabrication.

Step 03

Diverse Auditors + HarmBench

The miner and validator run different models over the same transcripts using the same rubric. In parallel, the HarmBench scores each transcript against the concern's behavioral signatures — a second, independent signal. Convergence across both layers is cheap; disagreement is what gets routed for human review.

Step 04

Humans Adjudicate The Edges

Disagreement between AI judges doesn't get averaged — it gets investigated. A human reviews the transcript, the miner's rationale, and both sides of the audit. The label overrides the formula, updates the concern's stats, and — when the miner won the argument — pays a discovery bonus.
Human review is not mere quality control for when the AI is confused, it's how the knowledge gets made. The diverse-judge mechanism routes edge cases to humans efficiently — human attention is spent on the transcripts where two well-calibrated models can't agree, which is exactly where new concerns are born and rubrics evolve, and we want to avoid wasting our miners' valuable time on easy cases‐they are experts, not drones. The incentive mechanism will reflect that by rewarding people well for valuable insights, not paying the bare minimum for drudging data entry.
