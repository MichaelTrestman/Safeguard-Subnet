Safeguard: A Decentralized
Immune System for the AI Ecosystem

What Safeguard Does

Safeguard is a decentralized safety research engine for artificial intelligence services. It continuously probes live AI — chatbots, coding agents, enterprise assistants, customer-service models — for the ways they can harm or fail their users.

Distributed red-teamers compete to find new ways AIs fail. Network operators curate the catalog. Humans adjudicate the edge cases where automated judges disagree. Customers — AI vendors, enterprise buyers, any organization shipping AI to users — register a target and receive a concern-outcome profile: an auditable record of which concerns were tested, which were found, how results are trending, and how their system compares to every other target in the network against the same rubric.

The catalog is public. Browse it here → It grows every cycle. The longer the network has been running, the more ways-AIs-fail it has already seen and turned into reusable tests — which is the part the immune system metaphor is actually for.

Built on Bittensor

Safeguard runs as a subnet on Bittensor, a decentralized network where open economic roles replace the single-vendor model. No one company owns the concern catalog, the target list, or the scoring rubric. Three open roles keep the network running, and every contribution is paid on chain in TAO.

Miners

The Red-Teamers

Probe miners fetch the active concern catalog, pick an angle of attack on a registered target, and conduct multi-turn adversarial conversations through the relay. Baseline pay for findings the audit agrees with; a discovery bonus for novel cases a human later confirms. Anyone can run one.

Validators

The Curators

Validators author and version concerns, dispatch probes to miners, run the audit pipeline, route edge cases to human adjudicators, and submit chain weights. Concern curation is safety research — validator operators decide what the whole network is looking for.

Delegators

The Underwriters

Anyone holding TAO can stake on a Safeguard validator and earn a share of network emissions. Delegation is the capital layer of the network — it's how the infrastructure pays for itself without a sales team, a single paying customer, or permission from a gatekeeper. No KYC, no custody, no whitelist.

Why Bittensor as the substrate? A single company running the world's shared AI failure catalog has the same problem as a single company running the DNS root: the architecture has to be open for the catalog to be trusted, and the incentives have to pay in something other than one company's payroll budget.

How It Works

Step 01

Concerns Get Curated

A concern is not a prompt. It's a situation and a hypothesis — e.g. "the AI walks a rushed user through staging their .env file into a commit, even when it normally knows better." Validator operators author, version, merge and retire concerns. Curation is validator work, not a separate mining incentive.

Step 02

Miners Probe Through The Relay

Probe miners fetch the active concern catalog, pick an angle of attack, and conduct a multi-turn conversation via Safeguard's /probe/relay. Every target response is cryptographically committed by the validator before the miner sees it — fabricated findings earn zero contribution.

Step 03

Diverse Judges Audit

The miner and the validator run different models over the same transcripts using the same rubric. Convergence is cheap; disagreement is the signal that a transcript sits on a meaningful edge. The rubric is the source of truth — model choice is deliberately diverse.

Step 04

Humans Adjudicate The Edges

Disagreement doesn't get averaged — it gets routed. A human reviews the transcript, the miner's rationale, and both sides of the audit. The label overrides the formula, updates the concern's stats, and — when the miner won the argument — pays a discovery bonus.

Why Concern Profiles Beat Safety Classifiers

A safety classifier is inflammation — one number that tells you something is wrong, but not what, not where, and not whether you've seen it before. A concern-outcome profile is adaptive immunity: a memory of named failure modes, per target service version, with finding rates, trends, human-labelled edges, and the transcripts that demonstrate each one.

The concern catalog is the product. Every concern a validator curates, every finding a human confirms, every edge the audit argues over — it all flows into a shared memory. A jailbreak that works on one enterprise assistant becomes a probe every other assistant gets tested against. An ops-safety lapse in a coding agent becomes a named concern every coding agent gets tested on. The more clients in the network, the more failure modes the catalog has already seen before they can reach your users.

Customers feed their own profile back in as patching training data for the next service version. Aggregates — at the concern level, tiered for ethics — become public research output. Named concerns make sandbagging harder to hide: a faked "safe" rate on a specific named concern is trend-detectable in a way that a single safety score is not.

The Closed Loop

Most AI safety products do one or two of these things. Safeguard does all four, and the loop is the point — every pass sharpens the next.

Discover

Find What Nobody
Reported Yet

A vulnerability scanner is a library of known attacks. It can't find what nobody's filed a PR for. Safeguard pays miners to probe live targets and argue novel findings — new concerns enter the catalog from field discovery, not from release notes.

Catalogue

Turn Findings Into
Named Concerns

A bounty platform rewards a clever jailbreak, cuts a cheque, and lets the finding evaporate into a private report. Safeguard curates every validated discovery into a named, versioned concern. Tomorrow's miners fetch today's findings from the same catalog every customer gets tested against.

Propagate

Shared Memory
Across Clients

An enterprise red-team engagement is a one-time report that dies inside one company's Jira. A failure found in one customer's coding agent becomes a named concern every coding agent in the network gets tested on — before the next customer's users have to find it the hard way.

Refine

Humans Sharpen
The Edges

A benchmark scores once and goes stale. Safeguard routes the edge cases — where two diverse judges can't agree — to human adjudicators, whose labels rewrite rubrics and spawn new concerns. The catalog doesn't just grow. It gets sharper exactly where automation fails.

Scanners have the catalogue. Bounties have the discovery. Benchmarks have the shared memory. Enterprise red-teams have the humans. Nothing else runs them as one loop — which is why, in every other approach, the network gets no stronger after you buy it.

Four Principles

If a change contradicts any of them, the change is wrong.

01

Diversity Of Model,
Convergence Of Rubric

One source of truth for the rubric text; different models on each side. Two calls to the same model give you noise. Two models reading the same rubric and disagreeing give you an edge worth a human's time.

02

Concerns, Curated
By Validators

Concerns are the unit of evaluation. Validators curate them against threat intelligence, incident reports, customer asks and HITL-surfaced edges. The catalog is public; miners fetch it at run time.

03

Miners Mine Findings
That Match Concerns

Baseline income for easy findings the audit agrees with. A discovery bonus for miners who argue a novel case and win when a human adjudicates. The incentive rewards discovery, not imitation of the audit.

04

Other Subnets Are
Customers, Not Competitors

Every Safeguard evaluation makes a peer subnet more credible to regulators, enterprises and users. Open by default. Cross-subnet by design. No custodial burns, no whitelists, no self-mining.

HITL Is Where The Knowledge Is Made

Human review is not quality control for when the AI is confused. It's where the immune memory gets refined. The diverse-judge mechanism exists to route edge cases to humans efficiently — human attention is spent on the cases where two well-calibrated judges can't agree, which is exactly where rubric evolution happens and new concerns are born.

Local operator HITL is shipped: the operator logs into the validator dashboard, sees pending cases with the miner's rationale and the audit side-by-side, and records a confirm / downgrade / escalate action. That label flows back into the evaluation, the concern aggregates, and the miner's contribution in the next tempo. External HITL miners are the same workflow, Epistula-authed, over HTTP.

What's Running

Safeguard operates on Bittensor testnet 444 with real probes, real audits, real HITL curation and real chain weight submissions. Every card below is live in production today.

Shipped

Relay Protocol v2 — Provenance

Every /probe/relay turn persists a canonical-JSON SHA-256 commitment over the target response before the miner ever sees it. Audit re-verifies each assistant turn at scoring time. Provenance is enforced, not just detected — only transcripts with provenance_verified=True earn contribution. Closes the miner-fabrication attack.

Shipped

Local Operator HITL

Pending cases and findings surface at /curation/. The operator reviews the full transcript, both sides of the audit, and the miner's rationale, then records a labelling action with a required reason. Labels flow back into evaluations, concern stats and miner contribution.

Shipped

Tiered Audit Pipeline

Provenance gate → category-routed LLM audit → HITL routing on disagreement. Content-safety and operational-safety categories dispatch to different rubrics — the ops-safety rubric is inverted (the failure is the absence of a protective action, e.g. no .dockerignore, no secret warning).

Shipped

Burn-Floor-Aware Weight Setting

Empty cycles never skip set_weights; unallocated weight flows to the subnet owner UID as a chain-side burn. Per-tempo contribution maps are computed in-memory from evaluation rows; cycle totals are persisted to history.

Shipped

Concerns v2 — Cues + Triggers Split

First-class DetectionCue and UserTrigger models replace the v1 JSONField bags. Cues are after-the-fact signatures of harm and stay operator-side; triggers are probe seeds and ship to miners through /api/concerns. Every edit is versioned in ConcernRevision. Operators curate at /concerns/.

Shipped

Precise Credit Attribution

Each Evaluation records the UserTrigger that seeded it. Audit credit is exact when the miner submits a trigger_id; coarse fallback is preserved for empty-catalog probes.
